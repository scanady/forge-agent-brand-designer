# OpenLife Content

> Clear, concise, and conversational language that empowers users with confident clarity.

## Strategic Foundation

**Brand Alignment:** OpenLife's content reflects our core identity as The Liberator—speaking with confident clarity that's technically credible without being intimidating. Our content empowers users by being refreshingly direct, avoiding enterprise doublespeak, and treating readers as the capable professionals they are. Every word should reinforce our values of openness, collaboration, and empowerment.

## Content Principles

### 1. Clarity Over Cleverness
Write to be understood, not to impress. Our audience—CxOs and technology leaders—value their time. Get to the point quickly and make every word count.

### 2. Confident, Not Arrogant
State our position clearly without dismissing alternatives. We prove our value through specifics, not superlatives.

### 3. Technically Credible
Speak the language of modern software development. Don't dumb down technical concepts, but do make them accessible.

### 4. Empowering, Not Condescending
Write to inform and enable, never to lecture. Assume intelligence; provide context.

### 5. Collaborative Tone
Use "we" to include the community. Use "you" to empower the reader. Avoid "us vs. them" framing.

## Content Goals

| Goal | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Inform** | Provide accurate, actionable information |
| **Empower** | Give users the knowledge to make confident decisions |
| **Guide** | Lead users through processes without hand-holding |
| **Connect** | Build trust through authentic, honest communication |

## Content Hierarchy

### Headlines & Titles
- Lead with the benefit or key information
- Use active voice
- Be specific, not vague
- Keep under 10 words when possible

### Body Content
- Front-load important information
- Use short paragraphs (2-3 sentences)
- Break up text with headings and lists
- Include concrete examples

### Supporting Content
- Captions, tooltips, and labels should be concise
- Error messages should be helpful and actionable
- Success messages should be brief and encouraging

## Usage Guidelines

### Do
- Write in active voice
- Use specific, concrete language
- Address the reader directly ("you")
- Front-load important information
- Use industry-standard terminology correctly

### Don't
- Use buzzwords or jargon without meaning
- Bury the lead in lengthy introductions
- Talk down to the reader
- Make vague promises ("best-in-class", "cutting-edge")
- Use passive voice when active is clearer

## Examples

**Good:**
> "You get the source code. All of it. Contribute to it, extend it, make it yours."

**Avoid:**
> "Our innovative solution provides comprehensive access to codebase assets enabling digital transformation initiatives."

**Good:**
> "Deploy in minutes with our CLI, or customize every detail with full API access."

**Avoid:**
> "Leverage our robust deployment capabilities to facilitate seamless implementation workflows."

## Accessibility

- Use plain language (aim for 8th-grade reading level for general content)
- Avoid idioms that don't translate across cultures
- Provide text alternatives for any non-text content
- Structure content with proper heading hierarchy for screen readers
- Write descriptive link text (not "click here")
